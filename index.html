Act 1 · The headline

It starts with one reassuring number.

Politicians and liveability rankings love to quote it. It is real — and it is the start of the problem.

The claim

The number everyone quotes

Across its rental stock, Vienna averages about 10.6€ per m² a month. For a major European capital, that is remarkably cheap.

The evidence

Cheap — by European standards

Set beside Paris (32€), Madrid (27€), London (24€) or Berlin (18€), Vienna is the bargain of the group. This is the story the city tells about itself, and it is broadly true.

The catch

An average is a hiding place

But one figure for two million tenants smooths over who pays what — and who can reach the cheap side at all. So let's pull it apart.

Act 2 · The split

One average, two Viennas.

The same headline pools a protected, cheap city with a new-contract, market-priced one.

Start here

Pull the number apart

10.6€ is the blend. The spread underneath it depends mostly on one thing: how old your contract is.

Contract age

Old leases, new leases

A lease signed in the last two years averages 13.7€/m². One held 30 years or more: 6.5€/m². Same city, more than double the rent.

Housing type

The expensive door is the private market

A municipal flat averages 8.4€/m², a cooperative 9.0€/m² — but a private lease runs 12.9€/m². Use the toggle to compare the two cuts.

Act 3 · The geography

The cheap city has an address.

Vienna's protected stock isn't spread evenly — it sits where the city built at scale. Here are all 1,776 municipal complexes.

Where the city built

Districts shaded by the number of municipal dwellings the city owns there. The deepest green is in the outer working-class belt — Favoriten, Floridsdorf, Simmering, Donaustadt — not the wealthy core. Hover any district for its count.

Follow the Gemeindebau

1,776 complexes, mapped

Each gold dot is one municipal housing complex, sized by how many flats it holds. Together they trace a century of social building — from interwar courtyards to post-war estates on the edges.

The pattern

The protected city is the outer city

The districts with the most municipal housing are exactly the ones a newcomer is most likely to be priced out of on the private market. Whether the cheap city is open to you depends on which door you can use — which is the next question.

Act 4 · The gate

Cheap rent isn't for sale. It's allocated.

To reach the protected city, you have to clear a sequence of conditions — and newcomers fall out early.

The queue

A line everyone starts at the back of

Half of Vienna lives in subsidised housing — but that stock was allocated under rules you have to clear from scratch when you arrive.

Filter 1

Two years before you can knock

You need two uninterrupted years of primary residence in Vienna just to register. Students and recent arrivals are out before they begin.

Filters 2–4

Then: status, income, a recognised need

Eligible citizenship or residence status, income under the legal cap, and a justified housing need. Hover any stage to read its rule.

No guarantee

And still, no entitlement

Even when you qualify, there is no legal right to a flat — and offers can take months. The people most likely to need cheap housing are least likely to reach it.

Act 5 · You

So — what's your price?

The average was never wrong. It was just never about you.

Vienna's affordability is real — but it is a membership, earned through years of residence and need, not a price open to everyone. As new market-rate contracts grow as a share of the stock, the gap between insiders and outsiders widens quietly, hidden inside a single reassuring number.
